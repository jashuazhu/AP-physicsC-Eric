Core Rotational Physics Concepts

By Zhu @ Groton School

1. Relating Linear and Angular Quantities

These formulas bridge the gap between how an object moves along a path versus how it rotates around an axis.

  • Arc Length ($s = r\theta$): Relates linear distance to angular displacement.
  • Tangential Velocity ($v = r\omega$): Relates linear speed to angular velocity.
  • Tangential Acceleration ($a_{tan} = r\alpha$): Relates linear acceleration to angular acceleration.
Key Formula Derivation: $v = r\omega$

The derivation stems from the definition of arc length ($s$).

  1. Start with the definition of arc length: $$s = r\theta$$
  2. Take the derivative with respect to time ($t$) on both sides: $$\frac{ds}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(r\theta)$$
  3. Since the radius ($r$) is constant for a rigid body, it moves outside the derivative: $$\frac{ds}{dt} = r \cdot \frac{d\theta}{dt}$$
  4. By definition, linear velocity $v = \frac{ds}{dt}$ and angular velocity $\omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt}$.
  5. Substituting these gives the final result: $$v = r\omega$$

2. Rotational Kinematics

When angular acceleration ($\alpha$) is constant, rotational motion mirrors linear motion.

  • Displacement equivalents: $\Delta x$ becomes $\Delta \theta$.
  • Velocity equivalents: $v$ becomes $\omega$.
  • Acceleration equivalents: $a$ becomes $\alpha$.

The standard equations: For example, the angular equivalent of $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x$ is:

$$\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha(\theta - \theta_0)$$

3. Basic Rotation Information

This section introduces the causes and energetic costs of rotation.

  • Rotational Inertia ($I$): A measure of how difficult it is to change an object's rotation.
    • For point masses: $I = mr^2$.
    • For distributed mass: $I = \int r^2 dm$.
  • Torque ($\tau$): A force’s ability to cause rotation, defined as $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$.
  • Newton’s Second Law for Rotation: The net torque equals the product of rotational inertia and angular acceleration ($\sum \tau = I\alpha$).
  • Kinetic Energy:
    • Pure rotation: $K_{rotation} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$.
    • Rolling motion (Rotation + Translation): $K_{rolling} = \frac{1}{2}I_{cm}\omega^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2$.

4. Angular Momentum ($L$)

Angular momentum describes the "quantity of rotation" an object has.

  • Rigid Objects: For a solid rotating body, $L = I\omega$.
  • Point Particles: For a single mass moving relative to a point, $\vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p}$.
  • Conservation: Angular momentum is conserved unless an external net torque acts on the system.
  • Static Equilibrium: For an object to remain perfectly still, both net force and net torque must be zero ($\sum F = 0, \sum \tau = 0$).
Key Formula Derivation: $\sum \tau = \frac{dL}{dt}$

This is the rotational analog of $F = \frac{dp}{dt}$.

  1. Start with the definition of angular momentum for a rigid body: $$L = I\omega$$
  2. Take the time derivative of both sides: $$\frac{dL}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(I\omega)$$
  3. For a rigid body with constant mass distribution, $I$ is constant: $$\frac{dL}{dt} = I \cdot \frac{d\omega}{dt}$$
  4. Since angular acceleration $\alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt}$, the equation becomes $$\frac{dL}{dt} = I\alpha$$
  5. From Newton's Second Law for rotation, we know $\sum \tau = I\alpha$.
  6. Therefore, $$\sum \tau = \frac{dL}{dt}$$

5. Key Problems: Rotational Inertia ($I$)

Reference: HyperPhysics Moment of Inertia

1.Uniform Thin Rod (Axis through Center)

For a rod of mass $M$ and length $L$, we consider a small segment $dx$ at a distance $x$ from the center.

Result: $I = \frac{1}{12} ML^2$

  • Linear Mass Density ($\lambda$): $\lambda = \frac{M}{L}$
  • Mass Element ($dm$): $dm = \lambda \, dx = \left(\frac{M}{L}\right) dx$
Derivation: Thin Rod

We integrate from the left end ($x = -L/2$) to the right end ($x = L/2$):

$$I = \int_{-L/2}^{L/2} x^2 \left(\frac{M}{L}\right) dx = \frac{M}{L} \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_{-L/2}^{L/2}$$ $$I = \frac{M}{3L} \left[ \left(\frac{L}{2}\right)^3 - \left(-\frac{L}{2}\right)^3 \right] = \frac{M}{3L} \left[ \frac{L^3}{8} + \frac{L^3}{8} \right] = \frac{M}{3L} \left( \frac{2L^3}{8} \right)$$

Result: $I = \frac{1}{12} ML^2$

2. Solid Disk (Axis through Center)

A disk of mass $M$ and radius $R$ can be thought of as a collection of thin rings.

Result: $I = \frac{1}{2} MR^2$

  • Surface Mass Density ($\sigma$): $\sigma = \frac{M}{\pi R^2}$
  • Mass Element ($dm$): For a ring of radius $r$ and thickness $dr$, the area is $dA = 2\pi r \, dr$. Thus, $dm = \sigma (2\pi r \, dr)$.
Derivation: Solid Disk
$$I = \int_0^R r^2 \, dm = \int_0^R r^2 \left( \frac{M}{\pi R^2} \right) (2\pi r \, dr)$$ $$I = \frac{2M}{R^2} \int_0^R r^3 \, dr = \frac{2M}{R^2} \left[ \frac{r^4}{4} \right]_0^R = \frac{2M}{R^2} \cdot \frac{R^4}{4}$$

Result: $I = \frac{1}{2} MR^2$

3. Hollow Spherical Shell (Thin)

For a hollow shell of mass $M$ and radius $R$, we slice it into thin rings along its surface.

Result: $I = \frac{2}{3} MR^2$

  • Area Mass Density ($\sigma$): $\sigma = \frac{M}{4\pi R^2}$
  • Geometry: Using spherical coordinates, a ring at angle $\theta$ has a radius $r = R \sin \theta$.
  • The mass element $dm = \sigma (2\pi R \sin \theta)(R \, d\theta)$.
Derivation: Hollow Shell
$$I = \int (R \sin \theta)^2 \, dm = \int_0^\pi (R^2 \sin^2 \theta) \left( \frac{M}{4\pi R^2} \right) (2\pi R^2 \sin \theta \, d\theta)$$ $$I = \frac{MR^2}{2} \int_0^\pi \sin^3 \theta \, d\theta$$

Using the identity $\int_0^\pi \sin^3 \theta \, d\theta = \frac{4}{3}$:

Result: $I = \frac{2}{3} MR^2$

4. Solid Sphere

A solid sphere is treated as a collection of "onion-like" hollow shells of thickness $dr$.

Result: $I = \frac{2}{5} MR^2$

  • Volumetric Mass Density ($\rho$): $\rho = \frac{M}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3}$
  • Mass Element ($dm$): $dm = \rho (4\pi r^2 \, dr)$
Derivation: Solid Sphere

We use the result from the hollow shell ($dI = \frac{2}{3} r^2 \, dm$):

$$I = \int_0^R \frac{2}{3} r^2 \left( \frac{M}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3} \cdot 4\pi r^2 \, dr \right) = \int_0^R \frac{2}{3} r^2 \left( \frac{3M}{R^3} r^2 \, dr \right)$$ $$I = \frac{2M}{R^3} \int_0^R r^4 \, dr = \frac{2M}{R^3} \left[ \frac{r^5}{5} \right]_0^R$$

Result: $I = \frac{2}{5} MR^2$

6. Key Problems:Rotational Energy

Rotation without slipping

For rolling without slipping, the key formula linking linear motion to rotational motion is $v_{CM}=R\omega$, meaning the center of mass velocity ($v_{CM}$) equals the radius ($R$) times the angular velocity ($\omega$).

Similarly, linear acceleration ($a_{CM}$) equals $R\alpha$ (angular acceleration). This condition means the contact point's velocity relative to the surface is zero, so the total kinetic energy is the sum of translational ($\frac{1}{2}mv^{2}$) and rotational ($\frac{1}{2}I\omega ^{2}$) energy.

Total Kinetic Energy ($K_{total}$)

For an object rolling without slipping, the total kinetic energy is the sum of its translational and rotational kinetic energies.

$$K_{total}=K_{trans}+K_{rot}$$ $$K_{total}=\frac{1}{2}mv_{CM}^{2}+\frac{1}{2}I\omega ^{2}$$
